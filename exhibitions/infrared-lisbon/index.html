Curated by Jaki Gerulskis

The city stripped to its thermal truths — heat and shadow where colour used to be.

Jaki Gerulskis has spent three years photographing Lisbon through a 720nm infrared filter, producing a body of work that renders the city unrecognisable and, in doing so, somehow truer. The limestone buildings glow white as bone. The Tagus turns dark as mercury. Human figures caught in motion become luminous, haloed — presences rather than people.

The infrared spectrum does not lie so much as it insists on a different register of reality. What the eye dismisses as background — the latent heat in old stone, the reflectance of leaves and skin — becomes the subject. Infrared Lisbon is not a document of the city as it looks but as it radiates.

Shot entirely on modified digital cameras with long exposures, the 32 works in this exhibition range from intimate street encounters to expansive architectural studies. Gerulskis brings the formal rigour of the photographic tradition to a city that has absorbed centuries of looking, and finds something in it that has never been seen before.

Key Themes
The Visible and the Invisible
Infrared photography makes visible the spectrum just beyond human perception, raising questions about what photography chooses to show and what it routinely suppresses.
City as Body
Lisbon's limestone architecture and dense human activity respond differently to infrared light — skin glows, stone flares, shadow becomes substance. The urban environment is treated as a living organism.
The Street Photography Tradition
Working from the tradition of Cartier-Bresson, Klein, and Moriyama while using a medium they never had access to — a conversation between the decisive moment and the alien spectrum.
