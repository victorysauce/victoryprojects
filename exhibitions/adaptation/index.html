A modern-day flâneuse — observing the everyday to reveal its absurdities, its contradictions, its quietly accumulated violence.

Anastasia Samoylova is one of the most distinctive voices in contemporary American photography. Her work moves across five bodies of practice — Floridas, FloodZone, Landscape Sublime, Image Cities, and Breakfasts — each approaching the same questions from a different angle: what does capitalism do to landscape? To the body? To the image itself?

Samoylova's unique position as a first-generation immigrant to the United States gives her work a doubled vision — she sees both the surface America presents and what lies beneath it. Her photographs are meticulously constructed but feel found, layered but immediate. The more one looks, the more the images yield.

Adaptation is the first major survey of her work, spanning from the early 2010s to today. Produced in collaboration with the Foundation for the Exhibition of Photography and first shown at Saatchi Gallery, London, the exhibition has been seen by over 40,000 visitors. It is now available to travel.

Key Themes
Climate & Capitalism
FloodZone and Landscape Sublime document the aftermath of climate disaster and the commodification of nature — not as spectacle, but as the texture of ordinary life.
The Gendered Gaze
Across Image Cities and Floridas, Samoylova tracks the ways advertising, architecture, and public space encode and enforce gender — and the small acts of resistance that push back.
The Photographic Canon
The Breakfasts series stages playful, affectionate, and pointed conversations with the history of photography itself — Shore, Evans, and others brought into the present tense.
Curator
Taous Dahmani

Taous Dahmani is a French-Algerian writer and curator based in London. She has written for Aperture, The Guardian, and Foam Magazine, and has curated exhibitions at institutions across Europe. Her curatorial essay for Adaptation situates Samoylova's practice within contemporary debates around environmental photography and the politics of looking.

New institutions may commission their own curatorial text. Dahmani is available for catalogue contributions and public programming.
